# Stage 1: Introduction and Motivation — Skies Under Pressure

The commercial aviation sector in the United States is a vital economic engine, enabling fast transport, tourism, and supply-chain logistics. Despite its critical role, passenger aviation is one of the most financially fragile industries in the global economy. This vulnerability stems from several structural characteristics:
- **High Operating Leverage**: Airlines require massive fixed assets, including multi-million dollar aircraft fleets, maintenance facilities, and dedicated airport terminal leases. These fixed overhead costs must be paid regardless of passenger volumes.
- **Exposure to Commodity Volatility**: Jet fuel represents the single largest variable expense for airlines, historically accounting for 20% to 35% of total operating outlays. Sudden spikes in crude oil prices can immediately compress profit margins.
- **Extreme Demand Sensitivity**: Aviation is a highly cyclical industry. Discretionary leisure travel and corporate business trips are among the first expenses cut by households and businesses during economic downturns, recessions, or health crises.
- **Thin Profit Margins**: Intensified deregulation and pricing competition have historically kept average industry operating profit margins in the single digits, leaving little room to absorb sudden shocks.

This study isolates and analyzes the financial and operational consequences of two major external shocks that disrupted the US passenger aviation sector between 2009 and 2022: the 2014–2015 global energy market price collapse and the 2020–2021 COVID-19 pandemic.

## 1. Contextual Background: Macroeconomic Shocks

### The Energy Market Shock (2014–2015)
Between June 2014 and January 2016, the spot price of West Texas Intermediate (WTI) Crude Oil fell from over $107 per barrel to under $27 per barrel—a drop of 75%. This shock was driven by a massive supply surge from US shale oil production, combined with OPEC's decision to maintain production quotas to defend market share. In theory, this price drop represented a massive cost reduction for airlines. This study tests how this supply-side cost drop affected operating margins across different carrier types.

### The COVID-19 Public Health Shock (2020–2021)
The onset of the COVID-19 pandemic in early 2020 represented an unprecedented demand-side shock. International travel bans, mandatory quarantines, and domestic health warnings led to an immediate decline in passenger numbers. In April 2020, passenger volumes at US airports collapsed by over 95% compared to the previous year. Airlines faced a severe liquidity crisis, forcing them to ground entire fleets, defer aircraft orders, and seek government intervention.

## 2. Airline Business Models Defined

To understand how these shocks affected carriers differently, this analysis evaluates 10 major US airlines across three distinct business models:

### 1. Legacy Network Carriers (American, Delta, United)
- **Operational Model**: Use a "hub-and-spoke" network. Flights from smaller regional airports route through a few large domestic hubs (e.g., Atlanta for Delta, Chicago O'Hare for United) to connect passengers to international and domestic destinations.
- **Customer Mix**: Highly dependent on corporate business travelers and premium international cabins.
- **Cost Structure**: High fixed overheads due to complex hub operations, diverse fleets, and unionized workforces.

### 2. Low-Cost Carriers (Southwest, Alaska, JetBlue, Hawaiian)
- **Operational Model**: Primarily point-to-point networks, bypassing large hubs to connect mid-sized cities directly.
- **Customer Mix**: Balance domestic leisure travel with small-business corporate accounts.
- **Cost Structure**: Standardized fleets (e.g., Southwest's exclusive use of Boeing 737s) and faster aircraft turnaround times, which keep operating costs per seat-mile lower than legacy network carriers.

### 3. Ultra-Low-Cost Carriers (Allegiant, Frontier, Spirit)
- **Operational Model**: Niche point-to-point routes, often connecting underserved regional airports with major tourist hubs (e.g., Orlando, Las Vegas).
- **Customer Mix**: Strictly price-sensitive leisure travelers.
- **Cost Structure**: Aggressive cost control, high cabin density, unbundled pricing (charging separately for bags, seats, and drinks), and low flight frequencies (often only operating routes on high-demand days like Thursdays and Sundays).

## 3. Project Objectives

This project builds an integrated, multi-tool analytical database to address six core research objectives:
- **Objective 1**: Establish a standard profitability ranking of the 10 target US carriers over a 14-year period (2009–2022) using weighted operating profit margins to evaluate business model efficiency.
- **Objective 2**: Quantify the relationship between WTI Crude Oil price fluctuations and airline operating profits to identify if fuel cost declines translate directly into margin expansions.
- **Objective 3**: Measure the absolute and relative decline in operating income caused by the COVID-19 pandemic across target carriers to identify which business models were most fragile.
- **Objective 4**: Examine the financial reporting gap between corporate Net Income and true Operating Profit/Loss during the CARES Act period to measure the distortionary effects of government bailouts.
- **Objective 5**: Determine the recovery speed of individual airlines following the COVID-19 shock, identifying the first quarters where operations returned to profitability.
- **Objective 6**: Establish empirical threshold ranges for WTI Crude Oil prices to identify the price points at which operational losses become common.
